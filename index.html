Environmental Pollution

What is Pollution?

Pollution is the introduction of contaminants into the natural environment that cause harm. Pollution can take the form of any substance (solid, liquid, or gas) or energy (such as radioactivity, heat, sound, or light). Pollutants, the components of pollution, can be either foreign substances/energies or naturally occurring contaminants. Although environmental pollution can be caused by natural events, the word pollution generally implies that the contaminants have a human source, such as manufacturing, extractive industries, poor waste management, transportation or agriculture. Pollution is often classed as point source (coming from a highly concentrated specific site, such as a factory, mine, construction site), or nonpoint source pollution (coming from a widespread distributed sources, such as microplastics or agricultural runoff).

Air Pollution

Air pollution comes from vehicles, factories, and burning fuels. It releases harmful gases and particles that damage health, cause asthma, and drive climate change. Using clean energy and planting trees can help reduce it.

Learn more
Water Pollution

Water pollution happens when plastics, chemicals, or waste enter rivers, lakes, and oceans. It harms marine life and makes water unsafe to drink. Recycling and reducing plastic use are vital solutions.

Learn more
Land Pollution

Improper waste disposal, deforestation, and chemical use damage the soil. Land pollution reduces farming productivity and destroys natural habitats. Composting and reusing materials help protect the land.

Learn more
Noise Pollution

Excessive noise from traffic, construction, and industry disrupts daily life. It can lead to stress, hearing problems, and sleep issues. Quieter technologies and better urban planning reduce noise pollution.

Learn more
Plastic Pollution

Plastics take hundreds of years to break down, clogging oceans, rivers, and land. They threaten animals that mistake them for food. Reducing single-use plastics and recycling can cut this problem.

Learn more
Light Pollution

Too much artificial light at night hides stars, confuses wildlife, and wastes energy. Installing shielded lights and reducing unnecessary brightness can restore natural night skies.

Learn more
Thermal Pollution

Thermal pollution happens when hot water from industries enters rivers or lakes, raising temperatures. It harms fish and aquatic plants. Cooling systems and stricter regulations can help prevent it.

Learn more
Electronic Pollution

Old electronics often contain toxic metals. When thrown away carelessly, they contaminate soil and water. Recycling e-waste and donating devices for reuse are smart solutions.
